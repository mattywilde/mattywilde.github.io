Individual Honours Project - In Progress

My Individual Honours Project developed and evaluated an automated system for restoring vintage audio recordings using discrete wavelet transforms (DWT) and transient-detection methods. The prototype was built in MATLAB and subjected to both objective and subjective testing. Below are nested sections detailing each stage of the project.

​

1. Project Overview

This project aimed to investigate the feasibility of a wavelet-based approach to reduce analogue artefacts (crackles, pops, hiss, hum) in vintage audio while preserving overall fidelity. A MATLAB prototype implemented DWT processing with multiple thresholding strategies (universal, level-dependent, adaptive) and three transient-detection/removal methods: local adaptive thresholding on wavelet coefficients, modulus-maxima verification across decomposition levels, and time-domain derivative interpolation. The system was evaluated objectively (LSD, SNR, ISD, MCD) and subjectively via listening tests on authentic vintage vinyl and tape samples.

​

2. System Design & Implementation
2.1 MATLAB Prototype Architecture

The prototype was organized into modular functions: pre-processing, wavelet decomposition, thresholding, transient detection, reconstruction, and post-processing. Circular extension padding handled edge effects during DWT/IDWT. The flowchart of the system illustrates this pipeline.

​

2.2 Wavelet Decomposition & Thresholding

A discrete wavelet transform (DWT) was chosen for its multiresolution time-frequency capabilities. Each audio sample was split into three octave bands (Low < 500 Hz, Mid 500–4000 Hz, High > 4000 Hz) prior to DWT, allowing level-specific processing without overfiltering. Multiple wavelet families were tested (db1, db2, db4, db8; sym4, sym8; coif4, coif5). Daubechies and Symlets offered a balance between time and frequency localisation; Coiflets provided improved reconstruction accuracy due to symmetric analysis/synthesis filters.

​

Thresholding Methods:

  • Universal threshold (λ = σ√(2 log N)): Assumes Gaussian noise; constant across all decomposition levels.
  • Level-dependent threshold (λᵢ = σ√(2 log N)/(i+1)): Reduces threshold in deeper levels (higher frequencies), allowing more aggressive noise suppression at higher bands.
  • Adaptive threshold (λᵢ = σ√(2 log N)/√i): Gradually decreases threshold across levels; balances artefact removal with preservation of mid-frequency details.

​

This image showcases how each threshold varies per decomposition level. Level-dependent thresholds drop more sharply than adaptive, making them more aggressive on high-frequency artefacts.

​

2.3 Transient Detection & Removal

Three approaches were implemented to detect and mitigate transient artefacts:

​

  • Wavelet Coefficient Local Adaptive Threshold: Within each DWT decomposition, a sliding-window moving average of detail coefficients computed a local threshold; coefficients above this threshold were attenuated. This method preserved audio continuity but sometimes left faint remnants of strong transients.
  • Modulus Maxima Multi-Layer Verification: Transients were identified as local maxima in detail coefficients at decomposition level 1, then verified at levels 2–4. Only those peaks present at consecutive levels were confirmed as artefacts. Although robust against false positives, deeper levels reduced temporal precision, requiring careful reconstruction.
  • Time-Domain Derivative & Amplitude Detection: A sliding window computed local median amplitude and derivative. Any sample where the derivative exceeded eight times the median derivative and amplitude exceeded 1.5× median amplitude was marked as a transient. Each confirmed region (±4 ms around the peak) was removed via linear interpolation. This method completely removed sharp clicks/pops, but risked interpolation artefacts in complex passages.

​

​

These figures compare all three methods on silent and active audio—showing that method 3 (time-domain) fully eliminated transients but sometimes introduced interpolation artefacts, while methods 1 and 2 achieved more conservative suppression in busy segments.

​

​

2.4 Wavelet Selection Rationale

In total, eight wavelet types were evaluated. Table 3.1 in the dissertation summarises each:

  • db1 (Haar): Extreme time localisation; best for isolating sudden pops but poor frequency resolution.
  • db2, db4: Balanced time/frequency trade-off; good for general denoising.
  • db8: High frequency resolution; better at preserving tonal content but less precise on transients.
  • sym4, sym8: Symmetric filters reduce phase distortion; improve reconstruction quality with minimal artefacts.
  • coif4, coif5: Vanishing moments in both scaling and wavelet functions; effective at preserving both transients and smooth trends.

​

3.2 Objective Metrics

Each variant was evaluated using objective metrics on six test samples (vintage vinyl/tape recordings). Metrics included:

​

  • Log-Spectral Distance (LSD): Measures spectral fidelity between original and processed signals.
  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR): Quantifies the reduction of background noise relative to signal.
  • Itakura–Saito Distance (ISD): Perceptual measure sensitive to spectral shape changes.
  • Mel Cepstral Distortion (MCD): Distance between MFCCs of the original and restored signals; lower values indicate better preservation of timbral characteristics.

​

Results showed that variants using level-dependent or adaptive thresholds on coiflets or symlets often achieved the best objective scores, balancing noise reduction with minimal signal distortion.

​

3.3 Subjective Listening Tests

Subjective evaluation employed paired-comparison listening tests with 15 participants of mixed audio experience. Each listener compared original vs. processed clips on key criteria:

​

  • Speech vs Noise (Q1-3): Was noise successfully removed from speech, maintaining vintage tones & speech intelligibility?
  • Music vs Noise (Q4-6): Was noise successfully removed from music, maintaining audio quality and fidelity?
  • Vintage Transient Removal (Q7-9): Which version removed crackles and transient artefacts with the least intrusion?

​

Results showed that methods combining symlet wavelets with adaptive thresholding and time-domain transient removal scored highest on perceptual clarity and minimal artefact presence. Simpler wavelets (db1) left behind slight distortion in midbands, while overly aggressive variants (coiflets) occasionally sounded "over-clean", removing too much noise and impacting audio quality.

​
